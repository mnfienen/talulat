# Views of Mt. Fuji - and that's why we take a shakedown cruise!

miles today: 48     
km today: 77.5

Route
<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_yamanakako/route.png" width=450>  

Today was a shakedown cruise and did we ever need it! Coming off being sick, jet-lagged, off the couch, we had plans to spend the weekend with Sterling, Geneva, and Elise at a cabin in Yamanakakako - a volcanic lake with amazing views of Mt. Fuji. Seeing it only being about 50 miles from Kunitachi, we figured it would be a good warm up ride. It climbs 1,600m over the course (no wonder this was the route for the Tokyo 2020 Olympic road race!) through a beautiful valley.

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_yamanakako/olympics.png" width=450>  

We were not the only ones with this idea as we saw quite a few roadies out for a Saturday ride, plenty of motorcycles, sports cars, even a couple Ferraris. It took a bit to pick through the Kunitachi traffic but we were also rewarded with some very excellent riverside cycle paths.

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_yamanakako/kunitachi.jpeg" width=450>  
<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_yamanakako/cycle_path.jpeg" width=450>  

So the riding was going well. Some shifting issues which are kinda inevitable after disassambling, shipping, and reassambling the tandem. We worked on those and made progress (which was good with all the climbing and needing to shift low!) and we got fueled with pizza-bao (thanks Elise for the rec!) and onigiri at a 7-11. So satisfying!

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_yamanakako/onigiri.jpeg" width=450>  

So far so good, but when we stopped for a minute to rest our legs, when I pushed on the captain's handlebar, it gave way (!?!?). Turns out, after 20 years and 10s of thousands of miles, that bar finally gave up the fight!

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_yamanakako/brokeassbar.jpg" width=450>  

My immediate thought, beyond being stranded, was remembering a racer crashing out of the Tour de France with a broken collarbone due to the exact same issue. If you are super dug in on your bars and suddenly one side gives way, all your weight shifts suddenly and you hit the deck. That's the absolute last thing I want to happen on a bike, but particularly on the tandem. We were only halfway through the day and just starting the major climbing. I really wasn't sure how we could limp along, but thankfully Chandra had a great idea right away. She suggested I shift the entire bar to the right so that the broken section would be within the stem. Awkward for sure, with the left side drooping down the right side off-center, but she was completely right! Even with that bracing, the break in the bar was almost complete when I looked in the morning. 

Our riding always involved issues - weather, mechanicals, calorie and fluid management. That all makes the pretty and easy days all the more sweet. But it's amazing having two perspectives on the problems and Chandra so often sees a wise fix right away. Honestly this one saved us from either getting stranded or, worse, a crash. So, thanks for the stoker mind of Chandra! But....the riding became more challenging. I could not really weight my left arm so I rode in this goofy manner with left hand basically on the stem and right as normal. It was really uncomfortable and, more importantly, it was hard to control at really slow speeds, so in the steepest sections, we often swerved a bit in the presence of a tiny shoulder. Thankfully, so many roads are narrow in Japan, and drivers (especially pros like taxis and trucks) have a solid sense of the limits of their vehicles. So while a tandem is not common, drivers are generally aware and while they get close, it's not very stressful to me. Chandra commented, though, that it's probably good that we've ridden to many miles on the tandem and, having lived in Japan long ago, we kind of expected how roads and drivers would be. Maybe the biggest triumph was a 15 or 20 second track stand at a stoplight early in the day.

Given this new reality, we got into a rhythm (slower than we hoped, especially on the few descents as we kept the bike super slow to minimize crash danger). The tandem definitely turned some eyes and we heard a few "sugey!" (cool!) shouts from the street and cars, and even one "Kakuii!" (stylin'!). It made us smile. 

We finally crested the climb to descend into Yamanakako and we were rewarded with the most amazing view of Mt. Fuji either of us had ever seen!
<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_yamanakako/fujisan.jpeg" width=450>  

We linked up with Sterling, Geneva and Elise at the cabin they had rented and it was a sweet homecoming, knkowing we would be able to fix the bike while still in Tokyo (this is why we do a shakedown cruise!) before the real riding begins. We had a great dinner of local noodles (Houtou) and Elise told us the story of their origin being to try and fill the bellies of hungry families, supplemented with leftover vegetables cuttings and maybe a little pork. It was great to see Fuji-san in the waning light then again in the morning reflecting the rising sun over the lake. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_yamanakako/fams.JPG" width=450>  
<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_yamanakako/lake.jpg" width=450>  
<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_yamanakako/fujimorning.jpg" width=450>  

The original plan had been for me to ride back to Kunitachi with Geneva while Chandra joined the others to drive back. I was stoked at the prospect, but alas, instead I had to take the bike apart - again - to stuff it into the small rental car thence to sort out repairs back in Tokyo. So now it's time for soaking up faraway family, playing games, and just enjoying time together. Biking will wait, but there will be plenty!
