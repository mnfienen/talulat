# Day three - Message from the rear 郡山, 福島 - 角田市, 宮城 Koriyama, Fukushima Ken to Kakuda, Miyagi Ken

miles today: 60      total miles: 214
km today: 97         total km: 345

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/route.png" width=450>  

Hello from the Back of the Bike (aka the Stoker)! I thought that it was about time that I said something about the ride, now that we are three days in.

Usually by Day 3, things are both starting to gel and starting to hurt.  

With respect to the former, we are “clipped in” to our road trip systems and communications. This is one thing that I love about riding the tandem with Mike. After tens of thousands of miles, we rarely communicate bike-things out loud, but just know when to speed up through a light or a gap in traffic, when to slow down, when to track stand at a red light and when to fully stop at one. And when we do call out things, it is usually something short and succinct, with “car back”, “car right” and “clear” being the most common. My checking for “car right” is particularly important here in Japan as the nearest traffic approaches us from the right side as Japan drives on the left.  

Signs, especially ones identifying a bike path, are becoming more familiar to us, leading to fewer stop-back up-turn around situations.   That said, we have been on bike paths wider than roads, and roads much smaller than bike paths along the way.  This morning, we started out on a beautifully paved path – it was the tail end of the Michinoku Bike Path that we understand to be part of the efforts of Fukushima Prefecture to build bike infrastructure and encourage bike tourism.  With narrow city streets and bumpy cutouts, the segregated bike paths are a relief although they often have VERY narrow gated entrances that can be tricky to maneuver through.  Mike does a great job at this, and I just try not to flinch as we squeeze through the gates (many of which I don’t even see until they are at my knees).

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/みちのく.jpg" width=450>  

And, it is nice to get back into our morning “stroga” (stretching + yoga) routine. It only takes us about five minutes, but it is amazing what a difference it makes when putting down the miles on the bike.  This morning, the hotel room was too small to stroga inside and then we were on the city bike path for the first part of the ride, so it wasn’t until about 11:00 am that we came up a hill and found a perfect place for stroga.  In addition to a concrete pad and picnic table, there was a pizza oven, which was clearly a sign that we should stop and appreciate the space.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/strogabasho.jpg" width=450>  

With respect to the latter, Day 3 is also when things start to hurt.  At home, we can get two consecutive riding days in on a weekend, but we rarely get three days in a row.  So Day 3 can hurt a bit in the wrists, the knees, the back, the legs, and most often, the butt (especially for me on the back who can not anticipate all the bumps along the way).  For that reason, Day 3 or Day 4 is often a rest day, but tomorrow will actually be a longer day for us as we will be passing through Sendai.  Hopefully, we will meet up with Rob from the Sendai Great Dane, but we are not sure if that is going to happen.  Stay tuned.

As for the riding, we had another beautiful day of weather and riding. For much of the day, we followed the path/roads along the Abukuma River as it heads towards the eastern seashore.  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/river.jpg" width=450>  
<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/bridge.jpg" width=450>  


The cherry blossoms are not as fully bloomed here as they were in Kunitachi, but they are definitely getting there.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/buds.jpg" width=450>  

About 20 miles in, the hotel breakfast started to wear off and Mike needed a snack.  He pulled into a “7 Holding” (a Japanese “7-Eleven”) for a “pizza bun” (a fluffy tomato-flavored (colored) bao with a tomato and cheese filling).  They are the 80 cent equivalent of the rotisserie hotdog one finds in the states, but better (no meat!).  Even better than that, Mike emerged with two oden eggs. For those not (yet) in the know, oden is a classic Japanese winter dish that simmers fish cakes, eggs, daikon, and anything else in a dashi broth.  It is lovely, and just imagine savory simmered hard boiled eggs at the convenience store.  Lovely.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/oden.jpg" width=450>  

Eventually, we left Fukushima Prefecture and entered Miyagi Prefecture, where we promptly avoided some major hills by going through some LONG tunnels.  It was cold in the middle of the mountains and slightly creepy as we pedaled along cars and touring motorcycles. The sound the motorized vehicles made (coming and going) was loud and surreal. I thought that I took a video, but it appears I took only a picture.  You can get the gist of what it felt like to go through 2+ km long tunnels!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/miyagi.jpg" width=450>  
<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/tunnel.jpg" width=450>  

When we emerged from the mountain tunnels, we found a pull-out next to the road for a break and a snack.  On one side was the highway, and on the other was a magic world of waterfalls and bamboo forest. This exemplifies so much of the dichotomy that is Japan - nature abutting engineered construction, temples sitting in the middle of rice fields, shrines tucked in next to metal collection facilities.  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/waterfall.jpg" width=450>

When we were on our Madison-NOLA trip, our route took us into New Orleans through some of the most devastated areas from Hurricane Katrina.  It was spooky to approach NOLA by biking through the outer parts of the city that have been abandoned for redevelopment as they are outside the defensible levies.  Today, we started traveling through areas hit by the 2011 tsunami and subsequent Fukushima nuclear reactors meltdowns, but much of the next few days will be along the coast where the tsunami was most devastating.  The area, like New Orleans, has bounced back and is evidence of the resilience of the people of the region.  Yet evidence and consequences of the disaster continue, and it is one area where tourists are especially welcome as they play a huge part in revitalization of the region. Most tourists though travel from train station to train station, so we feel honored to be able to travel on the smallest roads on our tandem, stopping at small stores, local spring festivals, seeing the farmers and just people out planting their gardens, enjoying the daffodils and other spring flowers planted everywhere.  
 
I would be remiss to mention the diversity of pathways a day on a bike takes in Japan.  As I mentioned above, we started on a new bike path, we rode through bumpy city streets, and through heavily engineered brand new tunnels. As we approached Kakuda (our destination for Day 3), the paved road converted itself to rocky gravel.  Hoping to avoid the bumpy gravel, Mike chose to go a few “blocks” (really, a few rice plots further) on the paved road.  Eventually, we needed to cross the rice fields to get to the other side, so this is how the day ended:

Even funnier was that just as we turned back on to the paved road on the other side, we crossed a motorcyclist coming up the road.  His head swiveled 180° as I think he thought he was hallucinating a tandem with two foreigners emerging from the rice fields! He stopped and turned around, so we passed him in a few hundred yards and confirmed his sighting was in fact real. 

We pulled into Kakuda around 2:30 pm so we had time to clean up, deal with the bike, wash clothes, write the blog, before we got hungry for dinner. Tonight, we are in a small, bike friendly guest house, called Guesthouse 66. I think that we are the only ones here on this Monday night.  Still it is nice to have a place where there are bike stands and tools.  On Day 3, the bike had a few tune up issues that Mike addressed easily (thank goodness for that shake-down ride to Yamanakako!)  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/g66.jpg" width=450>  

All in all, the bike gets better every day as Mike fine-tunes the brakes, the shifting, the random rattles, etc. Guesthouse 66 doesn’t do dinner, and after some researching, it appears Kakuda is known for beef and beef tongues. Luckily, we found a sushi/tempura place nearby that was delicious, even if they did give us a complimentary “service” of beef tongue.  (Because Mike ate my serving as well as his, I gave him the extra uni-shiso nigiri (sea urchin with shiso leaf.)  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday3/sushi.jpg" width=450>  


So, now at the very late hour of 5:30 pm, we are clean, fed, and settled into our room for the night.  The only thing left to do is upload this post and defeat Mike in cribbage. Current Trip Game Tally: Captain 0 - Stoker 2
