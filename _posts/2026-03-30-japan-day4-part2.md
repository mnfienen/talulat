# Day four part 2 - Stoker Post about things other than the disaster & guardian angels 角田市, 宮城 - 女川町, 宮城 Kakuda, Miyagi to Onagawa, Miyagi

miles today: 51      total miles: 265
km today: 82         total km: 417

Just a short post about the riding and things we saw **before** we tangled the chain, destroyed the derailleur, broke two spokes, shot out a rack bolt, and needed some prayers answered through Sterling, Robert, and Kumagai-san (aka, the bike whisperer).

Day 4 started out lovely.  [Guesthouse 66](https://road-trip-tohoku.com/service/guesthouse66/) provided a simple Japanese breakfast for us (rice, tea, an egg, miso soup) and the other guests (who consisted of a mother and her two young daughters).  It is sort of nice to have different accommodations every night on this trip – some are like tonight simple, but friendly and an extremely good deal, while others will be more of a splurge.  We were packed up and on the bike a little before 8, navigating along both the city streets of outer Sendai City and jumping up to bike paths on engineered levees.  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/brekkie.jpg" width=450>  
 
Since we were not able to make the timing work with Rob for a [Great Dane Brewery](https://www.greatdanebrewing.jp/) visit, we felt we had all the time in the world to bike the 51 miles (82 km) from Kakuda through Sendai to Onagawa.  Accordingly, we meandered through Sendai to find a local bike shop to pick up an extra set of brake pads (just to be safe) since we have some climbing and descending to do in some remote areas next week. 

We then stopped at Shiogama Shrine, a 9th century shrine (with a lot of stairs to climb to get to and Mike commented about needing a “leg day” (LOL)).  The shrine is a head shrine whose deity (“kami/jinja”) is the guardian of fisherman and pregnant women. I am trying to wrap my head around how one deity gets to be the guard of both fishermen and pregnant women. Is there some hidden concept of abundance or bounty with both? Does a pregnant woman's belly resemble a fisherman’s full net? Or is it just one of the unexplainable mysteries one comes across when traveling? The shrine is beautiful (as is its sacred tree).  It was worth not only the time to visit, but all the steps to get up to it.  Actually, taking the steps down was more dangerous in our biking shoes with metal cleats.

We then passed through Matsushima where a series of famous islands are considered one of three famous Japanese views.  They are beautiful, but we were unable to get an appropriate photo because we ended up on the land side of the road, rather than on the coastal side path.  This was the best that I could do by standing on the guard rail next to the road.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/matsushima.jpg" width=450>  

The most interesting part of the day happened as we started biking through the area that the 2011 tsunami destroyed.  We hadn’t really seen much of the damage, but as we crossed over a bridge, Mike spotted a beautifully paved and wider than usual bike path.  It was going roughly where we needed to go, so we made the choice to take it and see where it led.

We soon realized that we must be on an old JR (Japan Rail) train line.  A few hundred yards later, we came across a memorial sign explaining how at this location upon hearing the tsunami warning, a JR train stopped and evacuated its passengers towards the elementary school up the hill.  The tsunami hit the empty train, completely derailing it and twisting half of it 90 degrees, but all the passengers made it safely to the evacuation site.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/derail.jpg" width=450>  

A little bit further down the path, we found the Noribu Train Station, which has been preserved and is the location of a memorial park and commemorative museum. The museum is pretty sparse, but they have a short video with English subtitles that shows news and personal videos of the tsunami’s devastation.  I remember watching the news stories when it happened, but to see the pictures and videos of the tsunami in the place that one is standing in15 years later is hard to describe.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/noribu.jpg" width=450>  

Back on the bike after the museum, the path inclined steeply.  Mike and I were discussing that the incline seemed a bit steep for a rail line, when we came across a cut-out of the hill on each side of the path and an explanatory sign that this is where another JR train stopped, and instead of descending to a station to evacuate its passengers, a retired fireman advised that the train stay in the shelter of the cutouts at the top of the hill.  In doing so, the train and its passengers escaped the devastation of the waves that hit the area for over six hours.

Note the white signal post in both photos.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/hill2.jpg" width=450>  


I love Japan.  It is one of the many beautiful areas in the world. But at this moment, I am glad that we live in Wisconsin where the worst natural disasters that hit us are tornadoes and some spring floods.  A tsunami (along with a raging forest fire) is amongst the most terrifying display of nature that I can imagine.  When we collectively are changing the climate to cause more and more intense natural disasters, earthquakes and tsunamis are not at all something we can control. They are a reminder that no matter how much we engineer, invent, or innovate, nature itself is powerful and has the capacity to wipe out trains, towns, and thousands of lives.
