So, we started out our morning with a pivot - we had hoped to swing by the Great Dane Brewery (yes, Madisonians, there is a Great Dane near Sendai!). But alas, our schedules didn't quite align, so we took a more direct and shorter route through Sendai. I'd like to write about the Shiogama Shrine which was beautiful, and exploring some of the hardest hit areas in the 2011 Tsunami. But that will have to wait until we have more time tomorrow. The day ended in a way that could have ended the entire journey. 

A little background - as Chandra talked about yesterday, things are all clipped in and starting to jam. That includes the bike. I had routed the front derailleur cable wrong in all the taking the bike apart and putting it back together, so with that and a new cable, the shifting was way smoother and the full range of gears (triple in the front) was finally available. That all made the riding an absolute joy through Sendai. It was feeling great, legs were strong, navigation was easy. 

But a shoe had to drop. I made a bad shift (off the triple in the front) that on a single bike is pretty easy to flip back onto the chainring. I've taken some pride over the years in our ability to do the same on the tandem most of the time. Most of the time.... this time, the chain got tangled and was really stuck. Chandra tried to straighten it out but could not, so I broke the magic link, re-straightened, and put it back on. In that process, one of two things happened - either I did not get the magic link reset properly, or the chain got bent. I guess a third possibility was that the rear derailluer got moved a bit. It doesn't really matter, because just leaving that repair site, we shifted off the big cog on the cassette which trapped the chain between the cassette and the spokes. This made it slip a bit _and we should have stopped right there!_ but another seemingly light couple pedal stroked and the chain was welded in place, no longer able to slip, so the wheel stopped being able to turn, so then shit got real.

The force of two sets of legs pedalling when the wheel can't move has to be disappated somehow - so the things that could break did. The chain, but not before ripping the rear derailleur off the bike, exploding it, shearing off the bole holding on the rear rack, and breaking a couple spokes. We screeched to a halt (thankfull not going very fast), took a look, and realized there was no way we could possibly fix this carnage with our limited tools (not to mention parts!). We were completely stuck.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/disaster.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/stuck.jpg" width=450>  

A quick aside - on reflection, we have traversed the United States from West to East, over remote mountains and plains, through eastern Canada, all the way to New Orleans from Madison, and that remoteness is part of the allure. But it's easy to forget that, in a split second, we can be totally stranded! 

Thankfully, we in a populous area and I found a bike shop en route to our planned hotel for the night, and only 12 km away! Cool, but how to get there? We briefly discussed trying to make the bike rideable but just confirmed that was not going to be possible. So I tried calling the bike shop ([Kumagai Cycles](https://www.facebook.com/kumagaicycle) - his reviews on Google maps were promising!). I couldn't get through, but meanwhile Chandra called Sterling initially to see if he could maybe help us with language on the phone, or help us find a cab service with a van to get us someplace safe, or .... 

Well, his idea was "How about I connect you with my good friend Robert who lives about 30 minutes from where you are?". Such good fortune! He also called the bike shop and confirmed Kumagai-san was there, and Robert suggested the best bet was to get to the shop ASAP and have the bike looked at in person. Oh, and he has a van so he could come and pick us up and drive us there! And with fluent Japanese, he could help us navigate working out what we needed to do. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/phone.jpg" width=450>  

So, we layered up in the waning sunlight, and waited for Robert to fetch us. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/roadside.jpg" width=450>  

What a welcome sight for him to drive up to us! We drove over to the shop and found Kumagai-san there ready to have a look. Right away, he asked about the derailleur hanger (a sacrificial part connecting the derailleur to the frame - on our frame, it's a custom part and without it we would ahve been done for sure! But we carry one with us, so we told him). Then there's the issue of our setup being old and 9-speed. So he didn't have an exact match for the derailleur, but certainly one that would work. And a chain. 

All was seeming totally on track! But the spokes - he didn't have the spokes. We started plotting ideas - should we move two spokes from the front wheel? Should we try to ride with two broken spokes (these wheels are overbuilt and it was still true)? Should we try to ship spokes and tools to our accomodations up the road? 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/kumagai.jpg" width=450>  

Seeing this picture, I feel like it must have seemed like I was lurking! I couldn't help but watch all the steps along the way. My heart was sinking, but Kumagai-san had one more trick up his sleeve. I wasn't sure what he was doing at one point, but he rifled through some drawers, then returned to the wheel and started removing the broken spokes. Finally I realized he had new spokes! I'm not sure what made him remember them, but he was initially concerned that they were black, not matching the other silver spokes. We were just happy for any color that would keep the wheel round! 

So, in just a couple hours, we went from a non-functioning bike to a bike ready to roll! It was dark, and we were now just 15km from our hotel, but Robert kindly drove us the last bit so we could check in, get some dinner, and prepare for a short but rainy day tomorrow.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/theshop.jpg" width=450>  

I felt kinda numb throughout this whole process. I feel alot of culpability for the mishap since I do the shifting and I was just not keeping close enough eye on which gear we were in. I allowed us to get into a bad chain angle and then tried to correct it while on the bike. It was not particularly careless on a single bike, but on a tandem, the forces are different (we infamously broke our chain on our first ever ride 3 blocks from home in San Francisco!). I also meant to get spare spokes which we often carry but didn't get around to it before leaving home. Chandra gently reminded me of this and my heart really sank when it seemed no spokes would be the crux. But thankfully, that worked out. Touring on a tandem requires so much focus - on keeping the bike upright and functioning, on traffic and navigation. Sometimes riding style can become a lazy point, but I need to bring it back to the fore.

Despite my feelings of responsibility, though, Chandra and I did not throw blame or get worked up. We just tried to solve problems. Thankfully, Sterling was around, and connected us to Robert, and we found Kumagai-san, and all these pieces fell into place. Times like this it really does feel like a guardian angel is following us. Had this happened two days from now, I would not be writing because we would still be navigating a way to get us and the bike to safety. Again, we will need to be focused as we venture away from big cities and enter the rural coast.

Beyond generosity, it was fun to meet such a good friend of Sterling's. He has met other family and for us, meeting him helps paint a richer picture of Sterling's time in Japan, and for him it provides another touchpoint to understand a dear friend.

So we got to enjoy a late dinner, relieved to be on track to continue in the morning, and thankful to all three people who helped us immeasurably to be in that state. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday4/relief.jpg" width=450>  

One last thought about today to preview the rest of what we will write about from the day prior to our 4pm disaster. We are in the area that experienced the unimaginable disaster of the giant earthquake and tsunami 15 years ago this month. The location of the bike shop shows up next to green on the map. Why? Robert explained that that was a neighborhood where nearly 2,000 people perished in the tsunami inundation. It will never be rebuilt, but stands now as a memorial. So as rough as our day has felt, it's nothing at all compared to the immeasurable loss that happened here. Riding through the area, we stopped at several markers and memorials, and seeing this place firsthand even all these years later reminds that whatever problems we experienced today, it's nothing. But more on that tomorrow in part 2.
