In an ideal world, today would be a rest day, but putting the route together I couldn't quite make it work for this to be a no-travel day. It's probably for the best given yesterday's events - we could not have asked Robert to drive us another hour! So it was a day of some discovery ending in a beautiful onsen hotel.

The discovery was, first, a gingerly start to riding given the damage yesterday. Exploring shifting on the bike as if it's brand new. We threw the chain again once, but this time stopped before causing any issues, Chandra got it back in place, and we kept rolling. The new equipment works great. A funny side effect of the repair was the third brake on the rear hub got loosened up (in a good way!) but it means if I release the cable too quickly, we can snag or break it. Which we did. But this has happened before, we have the spare part and tools for it, and I fixed it in just a few minutes.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday5/fix.jpg" width=450>  

It rained off and on, but as a short day, we really didn't mind. It was cold, but climbing helped warm us up. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday5/raining.jpg" width=450>  

But speaking of climbing, I've been excited about these tiny roads around in Japan, and for sure we will continue to explore some of them. But today, on National Routes 398 and 45, we encountered hardly any traffic at all. The expressway tolls are expensive in Japan, so even though these roads are close to expressways, sometimes trucks take the local road to save the tolls. This can add up to some serious traffic, noise, and truck encounters (to be clear, truck drivers here never cease to amaze me with their skill and I'm not very worried about them hitting us, but I also hate blocking their way, and it's still scary when they barrel past). However, Robert mentioned that since the Tsunami, expressway tolls have been waved and so many trucks take those faster, more direct paths. Bottom line is, it may be safe enough to stay on National Route 45. This means less steep climbs and faster descents. Perhaps not as pretty scenery, but since we are trying to cover half the length of Japan with few rest days, it got us thinking maybe we should reconsider. The day after tommorrow, I can save us several hundred meters of climbing, 50 km of riding, but we have to brave some long tunnels. If they remain this uncrowded, we are thinking it's the way to go. Stay tuned!

We didn't take many photos today in the rain and short kms, but one thing we saw alot of is fishing nets and buoys (sometimes near abandoned busses) high on the landscape. These were deposited by the tsunami and they form a constant reminder of what this region has been through. This photo was taken at least 50m above the sea.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday5/debris.jpg" width=450>  

Other reminders are clusters of brand new buildings, abandoned busses and boats, trees that look like they were felled in an avalanche (up on hillsides) and others. We already shared some of our reactions and impressions, but the constant reminders reinforce the magnitude and I feel like we are both processing as foreigners who live far away and with a 15-year remove. 

Our short day of riding ended at the [Minamisanriku Onsen Hotel Kanyo](https://visitmiyagi.com/feature/minamisanriku-hotel-kanyo/). As an aside, this hotel was inundated up to the second floor in the tsunami, but the building remained solid despite the water damage in the lower floors. As a result, for months afterwards, it served as a command and housing center for the initial response. There are exhibits in the lobby with photographs and explanations of the role this building, their staff, and the community had in the response efforts. 

We were 3 hours too early for checkin, and we agreed to return at 3pm when checkin was allowed. We would hang out in the cafe (in our wet and stinky biking kit and shoes!). But a second receptionist came out and said we looked cold and offered us a day pass for the onsen to get clean and warm. I was delighted at the hospitality although Chandra joked that maybe they didn't want wet-dog-smelling foreigners blighting their lobby. In any case, sitting in the bath overlooking the ocean (the baths were nearly empty too in the early afternoon hours) was the perfect way to shake off the rain and to start our almost a rest day. The hotel staff were not all starstruck about the bike, but they also seemed to understand what we needed. They also insisted "for safety" that we bring the bike inside overnight to put in a utility closet. I told them the bike was dripping with water and we should let it dry, so we did, but when we finally checked in they insisted again and we brought the bike in.

All warm and bathed and with an afternoon to rest, we donned our yukata, played cribbage (I won....now 2-2), and Chandra worked on the blog.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday5/cribbage.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday5/blogging.jpg" width=450>   

At 6:30 we had our banquet style dinner which was amazing! Sashimi (including octopus, famous in Shizugawa bay, where we are, for their unique flavor due to a diet of abalone). Chandra was reading that, after the decimation of the fishing industry in this bay and concerns about radiation from the Fukushima nuclear disaster, the community here set out to rethink aquaculture in more sustainable ways. This dinner was a bounty of those efforts and was insanely good!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday5/feast.jpg" width=450>
