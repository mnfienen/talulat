# Day six - cold days are ok when they end with an ofuro!

miles today: 58      total miles: 355
km today: 92         total km: 561

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/route.png" width=450>  

We woke up early this morning to see the sunrise from our bed. Here's the sky at 5:19am - a little red. It was still a pretty sky and a pretty phenomenal view to see from bed. We dozed lazilly until 7:30 when the breakfast buffet was scheduled.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/sunrise.jpg" width=450>  

Wheeling the tandem through the lobby packed with people checking out of the hotel was quite a spectacle. We got all loaded up and then I remembered my big score yesterday. In a tunnel I found a roll of bailing wire! I stopped and ran back for it.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/bailingwire.jpg" width=450>  

Why does that have me so excited? Well, I kinda glossed over one aspect of our little shifting disaster. When we sheared off the bolt for the rack (!) since it ripped straight out I think it impacted the threads on the rack brazeon on the frame. So, while I was able to replace the bolt with a spare and lock it in, I'm not convinced it will actually make it. This bothered me when I woke up in the middle of the night the other night! I wondered where I might find bailing wire and - hark! The road provided. Pretty happy about that and hope we don't actually need it, but if we do, I have plenty.

It was not raining today, although my shoes did not dry out from yesterday. The sky was overcast with mist in the hills and cool - like 49-50F. It felt really cold but we both dressed for it so it was not too bad. I know I was a little light on the layers, but especially with a fair bit of climbing today, we kept warm enough.

We rode through a fishing village and it was cool to see loads of boats.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/fishing.jpg" width=450>  

We are definitely getting farther north here as the sakura are barely even budding.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/sakura.jpg" width=450>  

Once again, we continued to see both the demarkations of devastation and also recovery along the way. These things come together at a museum where we stopped in which a memorial to the earthquake and tsunami disaster is tucked in behind an enormous new seawall on a flat plain at the mouth of a river near Ofunato. Robert or Sterling mentioned this place was worth a stop and I'm glad we did. A massive stand of pine trees was wiped out, along with the loss of life and property. So from on top of the seawall, you can see a huge array of newly planted pine trees.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/seawall_pines.jpg" width=450>  

There is also a lone pine - called the "miracle pine" - visible near a destroyed youth hostel building. This pine was stripped of branches up to the towering height of the water and, while it died a year later, it is being preserved to stand as an example of resiliance.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/miracletree.jpg" width=450>  

Looking back from the seawall, you can see the museum building. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/memorial.jpg" width=450>  

Inside, there is some great geological nerdery explaining all about earthquakes and tsunamis and some history about previous known tsunamis in the area dating back to the 800s. There are also a couple artifacts, and two short films about the disaster.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/museum.jpg" width=450>  

The first film is footage of the tsunami hitting the coast in various communities that we have ridden through or will over the next few days. It's really hard to watch - an English-speaking docent came in at the end of the film and, seeing our faces, said "Are you OK?" She added "it's very emotional to see this." It's true, and if you've read the previous days, you know that we've been talking about this alot. I think it's part of what I love about traversing an area by bicycle. Had we been in a car, this would all have been a few hours, and I'm sure it would have made an impression. But now we have spent days moving through this area and continue to see evidence of the tsunami and various memorials. Not to mention the massive seawalls built to protect most of the villages and towns along this coastline.

Just little tidbits are moving, like a photo I saw at the onsen hotel of a building with the caption "this building served as refuge for 300 people who survived". Riding by it just north of the hotel gave it an oterhworldly importance.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/refuge.jpg" width=450>  

We stopped for a break on a climb and Chandra took this photo of a little pine trying to establish itself in a boulder. It seems symbolic of what we saw today.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/babypine.jpg" width=450>  

As for the riding, we tackled some pretty high climbs today - up to 300 meters. Looking at the elevation profile, Chandra said it looked like Papa bear, Mama bear, and a baby (in reverse order). Mama and baby were chill, but Papa bear was grumpy! That was a really hard climb with a long section of 10% grade near the summit. On the descent, hitting 50 kph (with the third brake engaged!) we got really cold! But thankfully that descent lead right to the Ryokan we are staying in tonight. This place doesn't have a website, so Namika called to make arrangements. Namika texted us today both to let us know our bike box arrived in Obihiro (excellent!) and also to tell us the Ryokan had called to ask our arrival time. We said be hoped it would be about 5pm. Turns out, we weren't far off!

<video src="../../../assets/images/jday6/ontime.MOV" controls="controls" style="max-width: 450px;">
</video>

The best part of the Ryokan wanting to know our arrival time was that they would have a bath (ofuro) ready for us. There is no better way to end a cold day than with a hot soaking bath! And, the Ryokan host insisted we bring the bike inside and leave it in the genkan. If you look closely, you can see the stepstool she found to make it stand up without blocking the doors. So great!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/tandem.jpg" width=450>  

Also great was the food, of course. We'd stop posting all these food pictures if our minds would stop being blown by what we are encountering! In good Ryokan style, this was not as much fancy as it was just wonderful home cooking. Not pictured is a potato carrot soup, a cheese-wrapped and fried surf clam, rice, and a rich fish soup. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/食餌.jpg" width=450>  

But, in case you think we are being too pampered, tomorrow's weather looks like this.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday6/weather.PNG" width=450>  

Temperatures ranging from 38-42. At least they backed off on the forecast for snow. Tomorrow's route is the one I was able to cut off about 32 km (20 miles) by committing to staying more on National Route 45, including some very long tunnels. We were on 45 most of today, though, and even though we went through a few cities, the traffic was pretty light. I think Robert's assessment about the expressways from the other day is holding up! So, it will be a long day and likely a hard day - more climbing than today, although no Papa bear. 

This first week of the ride is sorting out that we don't get a full rest day. Since we started pretty off the couch with little time to train for biking specifically in the cold Wisconsin spring, it's been a challenge. But thankfully, our butts have hardened to the saddles without getting any raw sores. Our legs are a bit weary, but strong. The abbreviated day of our mechanical disaster caused alot of stress, but did rest our legs. And then a short day yesterday and moderately short day today has afforded some more rest for our legs, but we have to grind through three more days (including tomorrow with sketchy weather and our first 100 miler the next day) before we get a two-day break in Hakodate. The power of baths and positive thinking I guess!
