# Day seven - sometimes it snows in April

miles today: 33      total miles: 388
km today: 54         total km: 615

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday7/route.png" width=450>  

This bike trip is crazy!

Having traversed the United States lengthwise (CA to WI in 2006 and MI to Halifax in 2023) and vertically (WI to Thunder Bay, Canada in 2010 and WI to NOLA in 2013) – not to mention the hundreds of day and short trips throughout the past two decades – Mike and I have biked tens of thousands of miles.  In those two decades, we have had a few mechanicals (including breaking the chain on our very first day of owning the bike just blocks from our house in San Francisco), but none as major as the TWO we’ve had on this trip. First, the handlebars on our shakedown cruise to [Yamanakako](https://mnfienen.github.io/talulat/2026/03/21/fuji.html), and then the derailleur-chain disaster on [Day 4](https://mnfienen.github.io/talulat/2026/03/30/japan-day4.html). And we have biked through some gnarly weather, but none as wicked as the freezing rain turned snowstorm on what was supposed to be an 80-mile day with 1400 feet of climbing (and descending).

</video>

After nearly five hours of riding up and down in cold rain, the snow started to fall harder. It was cold, but also very pretty, and Mike was a maestro in gearing for the uphills and keeping us in control on the steep downhills.

But . . . eventually, our fingers and toes got so cold that we pulled into a rest area that had this sweet little designated baby resting/nursing area.  We didn’t care what it was built for; it was warm and it was dry.  I think that our original idea was to warm up and put the hand/finger warmers Mike bought yesterday (thinking ahead) in our shoes and to keep pushing on.

But . . . it also had a tv screen streaming the weather conditions at four upcoming tunnels – and the weather was nasty at all four. The lower left (Tanohata) is the pass near where we were meant to end today. Totally unsafe road. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday7/roads.jpg" width=450>  

Some further research revealed there was a “Severe Weather” alert that included a “snow squall advisory”, a “snow accumulation advisory”, a “heavy snow advisory”, and a “frost advisory.”  Our weather app indicated that it was going to continue snow/freezing rain with 100% over the next six hours and that the 35° feels like 19°.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday7/precip.PNG" width=450>

With 33.5 miles (54km) biked and 47 miles (76km) to go, we were not even halfway to our targeted stopping point (and our reserved room in a local inn (ryokan) where a hot bath (ōfuro) and Japanese dinner were waiting).  Lets just say, we pulled the rip cord, did a little more research and found that there was a business hotel half a mile up the road.   Didn’t matter that they only had a smoking room.  Didn’t matter that it is a little expensive for the value.  Didn’t matter that there is a cancellation fee for the reserved ryokan.   Decision made. 

Here's the bike when we stopped.

But the day had good parts too, and so I will let Mike convey the best parts of the day.

Well, leaving the Ryokan was fun! The hostess and her husband had alot of questions about the bike and whether we'd be cold. It was raining, but we felt strong and confident, layered up well for the temperature and the rain. The forecast included a little "frozen mix" but the snowstorm was not part of what we anticipated.

We went down a hill about 2km and ran across a - well, it's hard to describe...our cousin Kerry connected us with Masa-san (who recently hiked the Ice Age Trail in Wisconsin!) who told us about a house of sorts, made from debris from the Tsunami, where people can stay for free. An English brochure deines it as "A Mysterious Tsunami Museum made from the debris" - called Shiome. It won the Post Disaster Risk Reduction Innovation Award in 2016. We weren't totally clear about what it really was, but having seen a picture online the multicolored building stood out among the typical neutral colored homes around. As we pushed by in a stiff headwind with driving rain, a Japanese man in dreads ran out to great us (in English) and Masa-san's advice that we would be welcomed there with open arms could not have been more true! We knew we were up against the conditions and lots of climbing, but we could not simply pass by.

Unfortunately we didn't do proper introductions so I didn't catch names, but the man, his wife, and their two young kids offered us sourdough pancakes and coffee. An older man - on whose land this sits, and the leader of the project, joined us at the table. The couple spoke good English and translated as we spoke with Ojii-san. It turned out they asked children, after the disaster, to draw what they would like a rebuilt village to look like. They all drew representations of the way the village was before. They gave us a book in Japanese explaining more, which we hope to read, but in the conversation, they shifted to explaining that the children needed a place to play and built this space from debris. 

They were all so welcoming and it was hard to tear ourselves away after a short visit, but we knew the day would be long. They said they understood, as the couple had spent three years biking from Alaska to Argentina! So we said, oh yeah, you totally understand that sometimes you have to ride through non-ideal conditions. They laughed and said they didn't have waterproof gear on that trip so never rode in the rain. Sometimes the shortest encounters are the most meaningful. I'd like to meet them again someday!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday7/house.jpg" width=450>  

Maybe the greatest levity today was our one stop in a convenience store when the rain was turining to graupel. We were drenched and leaving puddles every place we set foot. This poor woman working in the store was literally following us with a mop even as we made circles throught he aisles looking for things. We shared a good laugh with her while apologizing profusely and she kept saying it's no problem. 

Chandra already wrote about us getting into the snow and abandoning the day into this business hotel. As we were riding, she occassionally whimpered (right word choice, not a common occurence!) and I was doing the same internally. We were just getting really cold. My feet were completely numb and my fingers were headed that way. 

Once we decided to abandon, my body gave in somehow and I started shivering. I could barely write my name on the registration card at the hotel both because water was running down my arm onto the paper and my hands started shaking. Trying to put the key in the door was also nearly impossible with my shivering. So Chandra started a lukewarm bath and we jumped in. It was not too warm so as not to shock our bodies, and my feet were in so much pain I had to lift them out of the tub. The shaking and shivering continued. Finally, Chandra turned the shower head onto a hotter setting and handed it to me to hold to my chest. After 5 minutes I finally stopped shaking, and another 10 and my feet stopped hurting. She was so tender and kind and attentive. We were both cold, but somehow her care for me hit me deep. I love that we can take care of each other in such challenging conditions. She also said it looked like I had a Karaoke microphone and took a picture I am hesitantly sharing.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday7/bathkaraoke.jpg" width=450>  

Of course, not making it to our ryokan for the night has cascading effects since we've booked accomodations for each night. But thankfully we had a double layover planning in Hakodate in an inexpensive AirBnB, so we got in touch with Namika and had a google video call where she helped us find and book a ryokan for tomorrow night, got the next two nights of accomodations pushed back a day each (Thanks a million Namika!!!) and then we set out a new route, with a bit shorter days, and will use that extra day buffer in Hakodate to get back on track.

This hotel had a decent dinner and we even got a cribbage game in. It's now 3-2, captain leading stoker, for those keeping tally at home.

The forecast is for sunny skies and 50 degree temps tomorrow, so we are hopeful that the roads will clear and once we pass through the Tanohata Pass, we will be all at lower elevations with a clear forecast for the next week. Let's hope that holds!
