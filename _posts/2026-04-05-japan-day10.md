# Day ten - Can't get further north without a boat. 野辺地、青森-大間崎、青森  Noheji, Aomori to Oma Cape, Aomori

miles today: 66      total miles: 606   
km today: 105         total km: 963

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/route.png" width=450>  

Well, we slept off our extreme fullness wondering how Yoda fared. It rained all night but the forecast was for it to stop early in the morning. I decided I should really try to confirm the ferry and got online to do it. I ran into a subtle but problemmatic translation issue. Trying to book the early ferry for tomorrow, the English site said "tickets can be booked _up to_ two days in advance". So, it appeared, then, that it was full - problem! Namika to the rescue :). We got on a call and Namika got to the same spot on the Japanese version of the site and that same message actually said "tickets can be booked _until_ two days in advance". Big difference eh! So, she called them instead and got us sorted out. Thanks again, Namika!!!

The rain was continuing on as a pretty mild mist so we waited until 10am (short day today with tailwinds and little climbing) and headed out into it. The hotel was nice and let us keep the bike inside overnight, so at least it was dry. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/lobby.jpg" width=450>  

Definitely should have worn some rain gear - I got wet right away and we realized the forecast was off and it was going to keep raining a bit. So I geared up and we rode on.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/rain.jpg" width=450>  

After a couple hours, we stopped for a snack and while the sun wasn't visible, it was trying to break through, even evaporating water on this field.

<video src="../../../assets/images/jday10/drying.MOV" controls="controls" style="max-width: 450px;">
</video>

Another hour or so, and Chandra got super excited that she could see her shadow! The sun finally came out for real!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/shadow.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/sunbridge.jpg" width=450>  

The riding was pretty uneventful, as we left the Aomori Bay and returned to the Pacific coast. Now we are far enough north that the tsunami damage was not an issue and the giant seawalls have not been built here, so there are houses and buildings just across a road from the shore. We also noticed people wearing bear bells while walking (there have been numerous bear attacks last year) and saw a sign at a convenience store admonishing people to close the door as a bear mitigition measure.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/bear.jpg" width=450>  

Our legs are tired from 10 straight days of riding and we are both looking forward to some rest tomorrow and really just a ferry journey with little biking.

But today is cool also because we knew we would reach the northernmost point of Honshu. And, awaiting us would be a maguro-themed dinner in Oma - the town with the best tuna fishery for all of Japan (which is saying something!). As we rolled through town, Chandra saw these tako drying outside a building and wanted to stop to take a photo. But...I said we should keep going to the actual point because I was pretty sure the tako was drying outside the ryokan we were planning to stay in!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/tako1.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/tako2.jpg" width=450>  

We got to the cape and saw a motorcycler taking photos of his bike, so we exchanged taking photos for each other.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/大間崎.jpg" width=450>  

Then we saw this really cool monument/sculpture definitely depicting the tuna and the power of the sea. Chandra thought maybe the sea should teach me a little lesson.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/punch.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/thesea.jpg" width=450>  

Just as we were messing around there, someone called out in a combination of Japanese and English, not sure which to use, and he turned out to be Tatsu - apprenticing with his parents who run the ryokan where we were staying (Kaikyo-so 海峡荘). They had seen us ride by and thought maybe we missed the place. He took one last photo for us and we regrouped at the Ryokan.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/finale.jpg" width=450>  

It also turned out he had just returned (like last week or so!) from a couple years in Australia. He was really stoked to use his English (which is great!) and we were stoked to be able to get the whole intro to the ryokan and be able to ask questions about the dinner using English. 

Hungry and tired, we were happy to get a quick ofuro and then join the guys also staying here (they were enjoying a simpler meal - I think they may be on a longer-term arrangement) and it was fun as they marvelled at hearing Tatsu speaking great English with us and they shared some Shochu with us. But the meal - this was the best meal I've had on this trip and really on a short list ever! Unbelievable.

I did not make the same mistake of eating too much rice tonight, but got completely full on sashimi, tuna organ meat, a grilled tuna steak (melt in your mouth!) a stew with shrimp, scallop and egg simmered in a scallop shell. It was absolutely amazing! Oh, and if you look closely, something there looks like a bowl of uni. Um, it is! Tatsu told us uni here is renowned for its freshness and lack of a fishy smell. I could go on and on!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/maguroooooo.jpg" width=450>  

<video src="../../../assets/images/jday10/grilling.mov" controls="controls" style="max-width: 450px;">
</video>

It was all absolutely amazing, and Tatsu told us it's his dad who is the chef and he's self-taught. Again, while beautiful and expertly made, at the end of the day, this is home cooking. Sitting 20 meters from the sea. Amazing - thanks for being such great hosts!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday10/tatsu.jpg" width=450>  

Something else that really struck me today was the feeling of being in the North. Growing up in Minnesota, living in Wisconsin, Northness is part of our identity. And, even so, going "up north" in both states turns it all up a knotch. More open space, heartier people, more industry tied to the land whether forestry, mining, fishing, or the like. This early in the season, the sun can feel really warm, but a few layers are always at hand as the cold can feel really cold! And, well, sometimes it snows! We knew we would be taking risks with the weather, and over the next week as we make our way through Hokkaido to Obihiro, we might get really wet and cold again. But all that just feels like home. I love it! Tonight we are within a few tens of meters of the northernmost land on Honshu. Tomorrow, it's off to Hokkaido and, besides a great opportunity to rest for a day, I really feel like we will be discovering a new culture and environment to us. Let's go!
