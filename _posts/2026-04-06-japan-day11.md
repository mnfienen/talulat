# Day 11 - Remembering Dean Leeper

miles today: 9      total miles: 615  
km today: 15         total km: 978

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday11/route.png" width=450>  

Today is a rest day!!!  If by rest day, one means wake up at 5:00 AM, forgo any breakfast, and then bike 15 minutes to the ferry terminal, which is exactly what we did.  Thanks (again) to Namika, who called ahead and made sure not only that we had reservations for the ferry, but that they knew we were coming with a tandem.  We needn’t have worried, as we got there in plenty of time to finish our cribbage game from yesterday (I won!) and have some canned coffee and a snack breakfast, before boarding. 

We saw this sign at the terminal, which we were able to read as “Stop Customer Harassment.” From there, we weren’t certain if it was a PSA for terminal staff to be polite to customers, or exactly what was going on.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday11/har1.jpg" width=450>  

A little Deeple later, and we learned that the poster meant literally: “Customers Stop Harassing Employees”!  It advises customers to (1) Breathe, (2) Clearly explain what you want and why, and finally, (3) Be sure to listen. Pretty good advice generally.  Maybe a certain country’s president should try the simple 3-point strategy.  It reminded me of Robert Fulghum’s book, “All I Really Need to Know I Learned in Kindergarten.”

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday11/har2.jpg" width=450>  

By 6:30, the ramp lowered looking a little like the UFO landing in Close Encounters of the Third Kind. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday11/ramp.jpg" width=450>  

The ferry is quite large, but was not at all crowded this morning with only eight cars, one tandem, and perhaps 20 or so non-vehicular passengers.  Our route was from Oma at the very northern tip on Honshu to Hokodate, the southern port of Hokkaido.

There are first-class seats, but the “standard” is just a wide open, raised carpeted area for sitting or lying down. We spent most of the 90 minute ferry amusing ourselves by making origami cranes (Mike) and flowers (Chandra). The cranes are decidedly simpler, while the flowers are considerably “mendokusai” (translation: fussy, or as my Grandma Midge would have put it: “an incredible waste of time”). In my family, I am often referred to as the one who does things that are mendokusai (e.g., knitting, decorating sugar cookies, etc) which means I am also most likely to be assigned such tasks (e.g, shucking the Christmas oysters with a Swiss Army Knife).  But I digress, anyway, here’s a close-up of attempts #2.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday11/flower.jpg" width=450>  

We had a reason for assigning ourselves origami challenges.  

Our next stop after disembarking from the ferry was to ride to the Typhoon Memorial, commemorating the sinking of the Tōyomaru Ferry and four other smaller ferries during Typhoon Marie (sometimes referred to as the Tōyomaru Typhoon) on September 26, 1954.  The Tōyomaru sinking was one of the worst ferry disasters in Japan at the time (over 1,000 passengers died and only 150 survived despite being less than 1 km from shore). The ferry was leaving Hokkaido, when the typhoon’s strong winds caused it to halt its journey and drop anchor where it waited for several hours before it started to list and eventually sank. 

The Tōyomaru sinking is an important part of my family’s history as my Grandmother Midge’s first husband, Dean Leeper, (the paterfamilias of all the Leepers in the family) and a YMCA missionary was on board.  Dean was reported to have calmed the marooned passengers with magic tricks, and as the ferry sank, Dean and another missionary gave their life jackets to a Japanese woman and child. This was a story told among the Japanese Christian community in particular as a great act of sacrifice, courage, and faith.

Mike and I were unable to bring fresh flowers to the memorial due to the early ferry crossing, but we wanted to have something to offer at the memorial and hence, the origami cranes and flowers.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday11/mem3.jpg" width=450>  

Behind the memorial, there is a sign explaining the location and proximity of the five ferries that sank during the typhoon and a stone and tile installation also showing the direction of each ferry.  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday11/pointers.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday11/line.jpg" width=450>  

We followed the Tōyomaru line out to the sea wall and reflected on Dean’s life, and the incredible family that emerged from the Dean & Midge marriage (the four Leeper siblings), which eventually merged with the Ed & Scooner Miller side (the five Miller siblings) and now boosts 30+ grandchildren and great-grandchildren.

This video is looking out from the seawall, with a gray sky but peaceful sea. The Tōyomaru sank less than a kilometer out to sea. It feels like we could swim to it.

The rest of today is truly a rest day.  We are cozying up in an AirBnB Guesthouse, which we have all to ourselves, doing laundry, smudging the routes, tweaking and cleaning the bike, and generally just relaxing, as we have some longish days and some possible wet/windy weather coming up during the next five days before we end this journey on Saturday in Obihiro.

For all of you following the ride, thanks!  You feel far away, but we do look forward to seeing you all when we are back from this great adventure.

Chandra and Mike
