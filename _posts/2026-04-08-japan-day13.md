# Day 13 - Wind

miles today: 90      total miles: 774     
km today: 144         total km: 1232

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/route.png" width=450>  

Yesterday, when battling the rain, the cold, and the miles, I found myself thinking about Ernest Hemingway’s memoir, A Movable Feast. I haven’t read it since college, but a memory niggled at the edges – something about how being in a foreign country helps one better understand what home is and means. Until I read the memoir again, I confess that Hemingway may have written absolutely nothing of the sort.  Perhaps, I arrived at the concept when I lived in Japan just after college (when the memoir would have been much more fresh in my memory), and it is coming to the surface again, now that we are back in Japan on this amazing trip.  Or perhaps, I unintentionally made it up while searching for something interesting to think about instead of the rain, the cold, the miles, and how much my butt hurt.

But even if Hemingway didn’t write such a sentiment, I think that there is some truth to the thought anyway.  Living (or traveling) in a foreign country really does make one see home in a different way . . . home stands out against a different backdrop.

This morning, for example, Mike commented on how the construction we were seeing along the route must be the expansion of the shinkansen to Sapporo.  We’ve been across, besides, over, and under Japan Rail infrastructure frequently the past two weeks. And yet, passenger rail is almost nonexistent at home. We don’t hop on a train to go shopping, to school, to work, to a town over or a city a few hours or states away.  The innovation and the individualistic freedoms that “car culture” brought also squashed the collective efficiency for the masses that trains provide. Seeing the construction made me wistful that we had long-term public transit investments happening at home.  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_47.jpg" width=450>  

As cool as the Shinkansen is (transporting hundreds of people at 175-200 mph), I love the single car won-mon trains put-putting along connecting the small towns to the larger rail stations.  “Won-mon” is Japanese phonetic for 1-man, as in there is 1-man driving a single train car!  I had been trying to capture a picture, but Mike got one today.   

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_13.jpg" width=450>  

Another thing we saw this morning were wild calla lilies growing alongside the route.  I love calla lilies for their long-necked elegance.  They always seemed dressed to impress.  Yet, when we lived in the bay area, we found them growing in empty lots and next to our apartment building’s trash can.  And this morning, we found them (lots of them) growing in the ditches we rode past.  Although they weren’t tall stemmed (yet), they were still striking with their waxy white curls and a large green stamen.  Something fancy back home is a humble native spring wildflower here.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_44.jpg" width=450>  

Some funny things happened today as well.  This has happened to us on other trips, but we always find it funny when on the tandem.  Sometimes, the geo-location of our phones fail to sync and Mike and I look like we are miles apart.  This morning, at a snack stop, it looked as though I had biked 30 or so km on my own, while Mike was still sleeping at the starting point.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_4.jpg" width=450>  

We also saw a bicycle in a tree, which made us laugh because there is a bicycle in a tree on one of our regular rides just outside of Madison and because of that bicycle, Mike has a line in one of his original songs about seeing a bicycle in a tree!  I guess it is universal. Now we just have to find a Bathtub Buddha (inside joke).

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_12.jpg" width=450>  

Whether together or apart, we definitely are in Hokkaido: Japan’s Dairyland.

First, we came across this little snack stop, but alas it was not yet open. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_20.jpg" width=450>  

Never fear, though, because we had chocolate "Parma" ice cream bars and coffee in the morning, and then in the afternoon, we stopped briefly for a quick stretch, and found ourselves in the parking lot of an egg-themed stop called mothers+ where Mike found softo. (As far as I could tell, everyone else seemed to be buying 3KG boxes of fresh eggs.)

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_16.jpg" width=450>  

Today, we also had some spectacular views as we curled around the southern edge of Hokkaido.
 
<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_24.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_43.jpg" width=450>  

And although the day was technically our longest day, we had a ripping tail wind that pushed us almost all afternoon despite the fact that we first biked southish, then we rounded the Muroran point and rode northish.  The last 60 km or so were particularly windy, and it really felt as though Fūjin (the Japanese God of Wind) was pushing us.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_29.jpg" width=450>  

Two last things before I head to bed.

First, a call out to my sister Keiko and my cousin Katie who (like me) loves umeboshi (Japanese Pickled Plums). We’ve been looking, but again today Mike won – having done a snack run before dinner and found a bag of ume flavored potato chips! I am already pondering how I can get them home!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_17.jpg" width=450>  

And tonight, we went to a local tonkatsu place.  Tonkatsu is usually with pork but this place, also had ebi and oyster tonkatsu.  It was delicious, but our favorite part was getting a ribbed bowl filled with sesame seeds that we ground before adding the tonkatsu sauce.  Goma wo suru (ごまをする/胡麻をする) - which means to grind sesame - also means to ‘brown nose’, ‘butter up’, or ‘try to impress.’  You all can read into this picture what you want!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday13/2026-04-08_14.jpg" width=450>  

This trip has certainly been a “movable feast” as we have had 13 days of amazing food and adventures.  Hemingway was right about another thing: “Never to go on trips (on a tandem) with anyone you do not love.”
