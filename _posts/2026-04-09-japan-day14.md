# Day 14 - Transference - and how do you eat an elephant?

miles today: 77      total miles: 851     
km today: 125         total km: 1357

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday14/route.png" width=450>  

Today we rode into the wind. We ran into a loaded biker yesterday (concincidentally at a one-lane construction area where we were compelled to wait and he was the last vehicle through). He's the only other loaded biker we've seen and we exchanged a quick hello in passing and since we were riding along the tailwind, we noted that some days you get the tailwind and some days you push into the headwind. Well, today was our day. We knew for sure that we would be going into it today and we were mentally prepared for it. 

But we started the day out with curry! Namika had recommended ほっき(hokki) curry in Tomakomai and what seemed to be the best place I could find is open from 5am - 2pm. We confirmed with others that Marutoma Shokudo is the place to go. It's right on the dock, funky and amazingly good! So we got up early, skipped hotel breakfast, packed, made coffee, did stroga, and headed down to the water's edge.

The curry was so good! Super rich but not really heavy, although the multiple bowls worth of rice underneath was not exactly light. I had to help Chandra finish hers. What an amazing fueling stop for a big day into the wind.

With bellys warm and full, we ventured out onto extremely busy, sometimes 8-lane roads, initially north, but then turning gradually east then southeast - straight into a 15 or 20 mile an hour headwind. We knew we would have to grind and it would feel long. Maybe we overprepared, because it ended up not being so hard. We had a few not-really-notable climbs and a warm sun despite the pretty cool air temp (about 48 degrees). Thankfully, the grind into the wind had breaks, sometimes in the form of trees, sometimes buildings, sometimes we were just back off the sea, and later in the day the wind even shifted, ending the day with a mild but noticable tailwind.

In some ways, the scenery was more reminiscint of Montana and Wyoming (or maybe a little Kentucky) than the Japan we are most familiar with. We rode through an area that is clearly huge for thoroughbreed breeding. So many foals hanging around.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday14/horse1.jpg" width=450>  

And sometimes with the huge snow-capped mountains in the background.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday14/horse2.jpg" width=450>  

To our right all day was the sea, waves crashing and sun reflecting, and the the left were these far-off high, steep mountains covered in snow. We talked today how it's hard to capture the grandeur without a fancy camera, so while we have some photos here, we also had to just internalize what we were seeing. Time to enjoy the moment and let the view distract from the work.

The last couple hours hugged the coast closely and with a little tailwind, despite our sore and tired legs, it was pretty blissful.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday14/road.jpg" width=450>  

Chandra also wanted to stop and get a photograph of the first cow we saw, so sure enough - we saw a Holstein today!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday14/cows1.jpg" width=450>  

The wind noise, loud trucks, and having to pedal hard kept us from talking for long stretches today, but there were a few conversations. Chandra was listening to a podcast the other night that brought up an alalogy from lawschool that says "mice shouldn't breed elephants". The context is that allowing a narrow exception in law shouldn't also enable a huge one (using mice to justify elephants). 

This stewed around in our heads as I was also thinking of this concept of transferrence. I don't even know if it's a word, but it comes to mind as we ride sometimes. Like today, early in the day, it hit me that we started riding two weeks ago in Tokyo. Everything was sore and slow, the traffic and obstacles were a challenge. Ending the first day of a ride like this feels like nothing has really been accomplished except for seeing some sights and becoming sore and tired. It takes a few days to find the daily rhythm and before you know it \*poof\*! you are transferred to a new reality, 800 miles down, in another part of the country. On day 1, it feels like the end will never come, and suddenly you are almost there. 

It's that way with a single day as well. I checked the computer after the first really rough bit of wind. 19km down. 106km to go. It feels like no progress at all and then, with 19km to go, it's kind of hard to add up the pedal strokes and hours and calories burned that got you there. You've just been magically transferred in spacetime. Somehow I experiences both the trip and daily transferrence at the same moment today. I told Chandra that would be my theme for the day, and she brought up the elephant. It's like, you can't even measure one pedal stroke, or one bite of an elephant, but eventually.....there you are. 

So those are my deep thoughts for the day. We made our way to this funky guest house in Urakawa. They have an onsen downstairs (we each, respectively, brought down the average age significantly in the men's and women's baths). I was stoked to finally find a cold pool and a hot sauna along with the baths. Although now I'm warm (well, a couple beers and a very spicy Kimchi ramen (yes, pork broth - we decided since the ramen shop was in the guesthouse and we were tired, we would make this concession. And, the first legit spicy food I've had in a few weeks was very welcome!) contributed to that!). 

Tomorrow is our last long-ish day. We had plans to meet Namika and someone she's working with along the way, but he has to go to sea for good fishing conditions, and we are trying to beat a rainstorm forecast to heat up around noon. So we have some konbini foods ready, bags packed, and plan to leave by 6:30 or 7 tomorrow. Then the next day is a short morning into Obihiro! Transferrence!

Oh, but we got a cribbage game in. I'm on that way back into it! Stoker 6, Captain 4.
