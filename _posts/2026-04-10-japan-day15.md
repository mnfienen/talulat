Today was the penultimate riding day, and the last long day! The forecast was for cold rain again, but thankfully it looked like it wouldn't start until noon or a little later. So, we loaded up on some breakfast food the night before, got up early and were on the road at 7:00am. I did a little route investigation and found that, for the cost of one 200m climb, we could cut off the Erimo cape and save about 10 km. Since we were riding along spectacular coast all morning, we decided getting to Taiki before the rain was more important than going all the way to the cape.

So we set off from Urakawa with a very mild wind so right away started making great time! We had limited coffee at the guest house (Chandra had suggested we buy some at the 7-11 last night and I said "nah, we got plenty!" That was incorrect). After 20 km or so, we stopped in at a Konbini. We have been partial to 7-11 since we once found the elusive Konbini Oden egg at one which seems a month ago! Never again, sadly. Anyway, we've discovered the Hokkaido-based Konbini called Seicomart. Stopping in for hot coffee, I had a really nice conversation with the woman working there and her husband who had stopped in. It's still pretty shallow Japanese conversation, but we were able to talk about the bike trip, we had all lived in California at various times, and at least get a little below the surface. It was pleasant and the most natural conversation I've managed in Japanese on this trip. We also used the restroom and when we came out there were two bottles of water sitting on the bike (!). Assuming they were from the staff, I started pouring them into our water bottles. A customer came out and said ”私の水です” (that's my water). I was confused and started to apologize, but it turns out he was telling me _he_ was the person who was giving it to us. We had a laugh together and another quick conversation including Chandra when she came out. This all made us think Seicomart is the place to be! Everyone so nice!

From there, we put the hammer down and worked at covering ground. After a while, we saw the first road sign for Obihiro! Whenever we go on long rides, it's a cool moment when we see the first sign for the destination!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday15/obihiro.jpg" width=450>  

We turned into the mountains for the one low 200m pass to cover and enjoyed (as always!) a fast descent back to the sea. We encountered some draft horses. One foal was super curious about us, running toward us and neighing. The mother and the other foal were alot more cautious, nudging each other away.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday15/horses.jpg" width=450>  

On the eastern side of the cape, it instantly felt quite a bit cooler - this rain storm was clearly approaching. We also encountered many kms of tunnels (at least 15 km! - one single tunnel was 5km).

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday15/tunnel.jpg" width=450>  

Thankfully traffic was light so getting through them was pretty easy going, although kinda surreal and sometimes boring. With a reverb of at least 9 seconds, there was definitely some amped-up shower singing that went down. Here's a tiny bit of Simon and Garfunkel (should be spelled Funkle I think).

<video src="../../../assets/images/jday15/america.MOV" controls="controls" style="max-width: 450px;">
</video>

The sound of cars and trucks even far afield in the tunnels prevents much conversation in there. There were also shelters for avalanches (maybe) and falling rocks (for sure) for tens more kms. At one point, we found a cutout in one of the shelters and got a great view of the sea and stretched a bit.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday15/shelter.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday15/sea.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday15/sea2.jpg" width=450>  

At another stretch break, we happened to see a Kita Kitsune (northen fox) which is native to Hokkaido and Sakahalin (currently a Russian Island, but previously disputed). It was pretty cool and made a nice break watching it on the beach.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday15/kitsune.jpg" width=450>  

<video src="../../../assets/images/jday15/kitakitsune.MOV" controls="controls" style="max-width: 450px;">
</video>

After the epic tunnel journey, we saw these cool waterfalls and entered into Hiroo.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday15/hiroowaterfall.jpg" width=450>  

From there, we turned from the sea and headed almost directly north, up onto the Tokachi plain. It's no wonder that this area and Wisconsin share the sister-city relationship! There is a ton of dairy and related agriculture. It just feels similar with pre-planting springtime dark soil fields, the "dairyaire" smells, and farms. There is one difference though which is the Hidaka mountains rising to the west, covered in snow!

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday15/tokachi.jpg" width=450>  

The wind picked up with the approaching storm, but thankfully it was from the south! So we made it to our hotel in the early afternoon and managed to avoid a drop of rain! Now, we are warm and dry, sitting in the hotel, hearing rain fall hard on the roof and seeing it out the window.

<img src="../../../assets/images/jday15/rain.jpg" width=450>  

We picked up some chips, sembei, beers, and had a cribbage game (bad news for Captain supporters - the Stoker pulled away with a win, widening the gap to 7-4).

And just like that, tomorrow we have a short 50km day into Obihiro! There will be more time for reflection, but it's always bittersweet to reach this point in the journey. We've had this identified goal for a couple years. We've planned, revised plans, leaned hard on family and old friends (thanks Sterling, Yoshio, Kerry, and Namika!) and new friends (thanks Robert and Kumagai-san!) to get this far. We've worked through mechanicals and sore bodies to get to the point where riding 5-8 hours every day is our rhythm of life. On this journey, we also tried to relearn a language, and reconnect with the land and culture of Japan. It's so great to be in an area so different from where we've been before (Hokkaido) and to find familiarity (feels like we are home in Wisconsin!). We will find a good meal, get rest, and then tomorrow we enter another phase. It's going to be great to reconnect with Namika and her family and to explore Obihiro and a couple other areas before we fly back home. The penultimate day is a special and wild day. Now we just need to finish in good style.

Addendum - after another cribbage game, the Stoker widened the gap even more to 8-4. Captain is flailing in the cribbage world.
