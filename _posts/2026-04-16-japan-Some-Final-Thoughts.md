total miles: 954 (+ 48 if you count our Fuji ride!) = 1,002    
total km: 1523 (+77 for Fuji!) = 1,600

We have started our return trip experiencing the same magic of modern air travel - cancelled flights, rebooking, etc. etc. The difference is, we were already in a hotel _in_ the airport in Chitose (Sapporo) and it has an onsen, so last night and this morning we got to experience a couple last hot spring baths, saunas, and general rejuvination. We are still working out the details of our return flight (on the Chitose - Tokyo flight right now) but at least we know we will make it. It was a slow process to work with the airlines, but it really wasn't stressful. We did finally have to pay overweight baggage fee for the bike - $37 (6500 yen, which was the last of my cash - perfect!). We were expecting more, so that was totally fine! We are a traveling circus of baggage after all!

So, I wanted to share a few thoughts and post a bit of how we spent our post-biking time in Obihiro (and Otaru), getting to experience Hokkaido for the first time!

### Obihiro and Otaru  

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/lastdayvibes.jpg" width=450>  


Namika, her husband Hiro, and their two-year-old Haru were incredible hosts! We had shipped the bike box to their apartment and so were able to dismantle and pack the bike the same afternoon we arrived. It's always a bit jarring to switch from all the efforts to keep the bike rolling in one piece (especially after the mechanicals we faced on this trip!) to pulling everything apart and stuffing it into a box and a suitcase. It was super windy as well, but we went from biking to packed in a couple hours and then enjoyed an amazing meal at the apartment. 

Namika made lasagna with fish, miso and cheese - the combination of miso and cheese surprised me with how delicious and natural the pairing is! And Hiro went all out, reimagining the traditional Italian "beccafico" (fresh sardines in a breaded pouch) using more local ingredients - he called it "sabafico" since he replaced the sardines with Japanese cured mackeral (shime saba). He also adapted the filling using walnuts rather than pinenuts, but still with raisins. Instead of breading, he stuffed these into aburage (fried tofu skin). It was amazing! Innovative and delicious. For young Haru, though, I think the star of the show was a crab brought by another guest (a konbu farmer and fisherman), Hoshi-san, who had caught it earlier in the day.

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/haru.jpg" width=450>  

And this was just the first night! We cooked together again the next night - Okonomiyaki with some definite lessons to up our game at home! The third day in Obihiro, we learned to make tofu (a local tofu maker gave us a private lesson with Namika - it was amazing!). Then it was their favorite French restaurant which was a great meal of French techniques, local ingredients, and fantastic wine accompaniments. The early asparagus here are giant but tender, accompanied by a scallop and three sauces.

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/asp.jpg" width=450>  

Local salmon in a lemon cream sauce was topped with mountain vegetables from nearby, including fiddle fern heads. The chef is local and trained in France. What an amazing treat!

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/salmon.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/chef.jpg" width=450>  

I know it sounds like all I'm talking about is food in Obihiro, but being off the bike and hanging out with fellow lovers of good food and wine, it was a big focus of our time.

But we also got to tour around town, do some shopping for omiyage, and also checked out the local horseracing style. This is a form where huge horses (like Clydsedale size) drag sleds through gravel, including up and over two major obstacles. It's so difficult that the horses have to rest between the two humps. It's amazing to see so much power and the strategy is pretty fascinating. In this photo, you can see how some horses made it over the first hump easily (#4 ultimately won) while the one closest to us didn't make it over at all and had to abandon.  

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/horses.jpg" width=450>  

When the latest group of students from Obihiro was in Madison as part of the sister city exchange, we got to meet their English teacher who invited us to come to the Nansho high school. We met the students, did our best to make formal introductions to the principal and teachers in Japanese, and gave a presentation to the English class (in English). The students were shy, but in the end asked some really great questions (including "What is your favorite Japanese word?" and "What do you like best about each other?").  

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/five.jpg" width=450>  

This was followed by a live calligraphy performance from the calligraphy club complete with choir accompaniment! The script says "Chandra-san, Mike-san, welcome to Obihiro." It was quite a production and really fun!

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/shuji1.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/shuji2.jpg" width=450>  

From there, Namika and Hiro rented a van so we could all go to the Sea of Japan coast near Sapporo to a cool formerly principle port called Otaru. We drove through the mountains we had initially planned as our biking route. I think we'll have to return to explore the mountains more - maybe a bit later like May or June.  

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/山々.jpg" width=450>  

In route planning, we found an Ainu cultural center that Namika and Hiro also had not seen before, so we made a stop at the [Nibutani Ainu Culture Museum](https://www.biratori-ainu-culture.com/en/trip/nibutani-museum/). The contruction techniques for the style of dwellings and structures that the indigenous Ainu people of Japan made and lived in were lost, but somewhat recently an artisan in this area reverse engineered the techniques and built a small group of buildings accompanying the museum. The museum had many garments, tools, weapons, and religious artifacts. It would be cool to come back with a docent sometime.  

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/ainu_dwelling.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/ainuboat.jpg" width=450>  

Finally we made it to Otaru where we strolled some old stone buildings that are now shops (many sweets and omiyage) and the old canal with European influenced architecture from about 1900. The canal was built before port modernization to allow smaller boats to offload goods from oceangoing ships to warehouses along the canal. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/canal.jpg" width=450>  

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/mc.jpg" width=450>  

Our final stop was the Nikka Whiskey distillery in nearby Yoichi where Masataka Taketsuru pioneered Scotch Whiskey techniques in Japan. It was a fascinating history both of the company and of Taketsuru-san and his Scottish wife Rita. The whiskey was pretty tasty as well! 

From there, we went to the New Chitose airport near Sapporo to start the journey home. We had so much fun with Namika, Hiro, and Haru but had to say goodbye. 

<img src="../../../assets/images/japan_final/byebye.jpg" width=450>  

There's and hotel and an onsen (and plenty of restaurants etc.) _in_ the airport, so we got in a couple final baths/saunas, a good night sleep, and were ready to start heading home. Our flight to Tokyo got cancelled, and there was some epic standing at counters with mostly English and a little Japanese trying to get rescheduled. The details aren't that interesting, but it will end up being 35 or so hours in airports, but at least we are now flying all the way to Madison rather than the original plan of Chicago with getting picked up for a long late-night drive.

### Some last thoughts 

This was not at all our first long-distance tandem ride, but for sure we learned some new things. We got insanely lucky with the two big mechanical issues we experienced and were able to keep moving without real delay, thanks to a combination of timing and amazing help from friends & family old and new. But this highlighted to me a need to really reassess the bike. It's 20 years old, some parts are out of date (we got a little lucky that Kumagai-san had a derailleur that worked with our setup), and the wear on others (like the handlebars!) can be hard to see, but we need to dig a little deeper and do some modernization.

We also knew the roads in Japan would be steep, but I think I underestimated just _how_ steep some would be on the tandem. Particularly with the shifting issues (and consequences!) on day 4, we had to be extra gentle shifting. Combining that with the weather we encountered, we adjusted the route to avoid some of the Rin-do I was excited to ride on in favor of less steep (but still steep!) larger roads. Traffic was never a real issue, but sometimes we missed some forest that would have been cool to see. But like in Canada a couple years ago, the combination of RideWithGPS for planning and the long-battery Wahoo ELEMNT computer for navigation, we were able to make some adjustments to the route easily.

This is the first time we have not been camping on a trip like this. I think we both missed the vibe of camping at night, but the amazing food and hospitality we enjoyed in both hotels and traditional inns was part of what made the experience amazing. I don't think this means we don't camp in the future on journeys like this, but for sure in Japan the infrastructure and reasonable expense (partly because of the strong dollar to yen exchange of late) made it the way to go! Plus, we were too early in the season for most campgrounds anyway....

Every time we make a long bike journey, it is satisfying to maybe drive the route in the future, or even just see the distance on a map. This time, we flew from New Chitose to Tokyo largely retracing the route. That it took nearly two hours on a jet airliner was a real measuring stick. We covered some real ground!

It was indescribably helpful to have friends and family who could help us navigate some accomodations over the phone in Japanese. It was also really fun to reconnect with Japanese language communication together as we went. We remain novices, but with patience on the part of all parties, we navigated pretty well and as words, phrases, writing, and grammar all bubbled up from deep recesses in our minds, we felt really connected to Japan. We didn't live there long, but the time we spent there was foundational for our relationship and for us as individuals - alot is packed in!

But finally, the best part and true privilege of this time is a month exploring together. The connections with family and friends was wonderful and important, but the Stoked-Captian connections are golden. We were working every day toward a common goal. We each contributed in different ways every day and accomplished alot as a team. We are blessed to even want to do these things together, and also to be able to work together so well. As Yoshio said in another text, "It's important to do hard things!". Indeed, some suffering and overcoming makes us more whole and brings us together. So, until next time, this experience will be another set of deep memories we will share and encounter for years to come.
